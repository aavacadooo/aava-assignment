THE TALE OF THE BLUE TOOTH: HOW A SWEDISH KING AND A WIRELESS DREAM CHANGED THE WORLD

Imagine it’s the mid-1990s. In 1994, engineers Jaap Haartsen and Sven Mattisson at Sweden's Ericsson tackled a persistent challenge: eliminating wired connections and cables between mobile devices. The goal was to strategically develop this innovation and standardize it across industries to turn it into a global connectivity standard.

The name "Bluetooth" was proposed in 1997, inspired by the 10th-century Scandinavian king, Harald "Bluetooth" Gormsson, who successfully united Denmark and Norway. The creators chose this name to symbolize the technology's ability to unite the fragmented worlds of PC and cellular industries! A fascinating part of this history remains in every smartphone today: the iconic Bluetooth logo is actually a Viking bindrune, merging the runes for Harald’s initials: Hagall (ᚼ) and Bjarkan (ᛒ).

Uncertainty in innovation

Jumping into unproven wireless technology was risky, demonstrating the classic dilemma of innovation management. Bluetooth’s journey is a classic study of Pearson’s Uncertainty Map, specifically starting in Applications Engineering, where the technology of short-range radio frequency was understood, but the specific market applications were still being discovered.

Dynamic Capabilities

Ericsson had to balance Exploitation to leverage their existing expertise in cellular technology with Exploration, seeking entirely new ways to connect disparate hardware.

Uniting the Tribes

For Bluetooth to be a true success, it couldn't belong to just one company. In May 1998, a Special Interest Group (SIG) was established. This open Global Standard allowed the technology to achieve rapid Technology Diffusion, across global markets. This strategic move allowed Bluetooth to evolve from a simple mobile headset connector into a global standard for the Internet of Things (IoT) and medical devices.

By mastering the innovation process, the developers successfully standardized Bluetooth across industries to turn it into a global connectivity standard!
